# Iron Salt

## Chapter One — The Bell Under the Hill

Tavin broke the rat’s back with the flat of his shovel and dropped the twitching thing into the furnace trough.

The heat slapped his face hard enough to sting the corners of his eyes. Fat popped inside the coals. The furnace room smelled of wet ash, iron filings, old sweat soaked into leather aprons, and the sharp sour bite of alchemical salts. Somebody upstairs had spilled cabbage broth in the corridor again. The stink drifted down the stairwell every time the kitchen door opened.

“Move your feet,” Old Neral said.

Tavin dragged the ore cart aside before the next chain bucket came rattling down the chute. The pulley screamed above them. Dust drifted from the beams. The whole underforge groaned day and night like a ship trapped beneath black water.

He had worked under Mount Erset since he was eleven years old. He knew every sound in the forge district: the cough before a steam pipe burst, the hollow ring of cracked iron, the thin scream a man made when molten slag got through his boot.

The bell beneath the hill was new.

It rang once.

Every hammer in the room stopped.

Neral looked up first. His bad eye wandered toward the ceiling while the good one fixed on the western tunnel.

The bell rang again.

Not loud. Heavy.

The sound carried through stone instead of air.

Tavin felt it in his teeth.

Nobody spoke for three breaths.

Then Kess, one of the coal boys, whispered, “Gate bell.”

Neral smacked him across the back of the head. “Shut your mouth.”

But men had already started muttering.

Gate bell.

Deep roads.

The sealed stair.

Tavin wiped soot across his forehead with the back of his wrist. “I thought the western gate was buried.”

“It is.” Neral shoved another ore pan toward the furnace. “Work.”

The bell rang a third time.

Closer.

Somewhere far back in the tunnels, metal ground against stone.

Tavin’s stomach tightened.

The old stories drifted around the workers every winter after too much rotbeer. Stone kings sleeping under the mountain. Bronze doors sealed with blood. The dead walking old roads carrying lamps full of green fire.

Most of the forge crews laughed at those stories.

None of them laughed now.

A runner came hammering down the stairs from the upper foundry. He wore the city crest on his chest. White linen gone grey at the cuffs. One boot half unlaced.

“Seal the lower vents,” he shouted. “Guild order. Every western passage closed. Nobody leaves the district.”

Neral spat into the ash pit. “What happened?”

“Road wardens missing.”

The runner’s face glistened with sweat despite the cold coming off the stairwell.

“Three teams. The governor wants every laborer counted.”

“Missing where?” Tavin asked.

The runner looked at him like he’d forgotten forge boys could talk.

“Old cistern road.”

The room went still again.

Everybody knew the cistern road.

Nobody used it.

The western tunnels had been sealed forty years earlier after the flood wars. The city elders said poison gas seeped from the deep chambers. A few smugglers still tried the routes now and then. Most came back coughing blood.

Some never came back.

The runner pointed at Tavin. “You. Carrying arms?”

Tavin shook his head.

“You can swing a pick?”

“I work forge haul.”

“Good enough. Report topside.”

Neral barked a laugh. “He’s sixteen.”

“Then he’s narrow enough for tunnel work.”

The runner shoved a wax-marked slate into Tavin’s hands and moved to the next room.

Tavin stared after him.

Neral scratched at the burn scars along his jaw. “Bad luck, boy.”

“I didn’t volunteer.”

“Doesn’t matter.”

The old man leaned closer. Tavin smelled coal smoke trapped in his beard.

“When city men start pulling workers off shift, something already went wrong.”

Tavin climbed out of the furnace district twenty minutes later carrying a rusted short spear and a leather water flask that smelled faintly of vinegar.

The upper streets of Kel Harrow sat under freezing rain.

Smoke hung low between the buildings.

Water dripped from iron gutters into black channels cut through the stone roads. Traders shouted under canvas awnings. Oxen hauled coal sledges uphill toward the manufactories. The city climbed the mountain in layers: furnace wards at the bottom, trade terraces above them, then the old fortress district built straight into the cliffs.

Everything in Kel Harrow smelled used.

Grease.

Coal.

Horse piss.

Cheap soap.

Wet wool.

Tavin loved it anyway.

He crossed Hammer Court toward the militia barracks and nearly collided with his sister.

Mira grabbed his sleeve. “Where are you going?”

She carried a basket of laundry against one hip. Rain darkened the curls around her temples. Her hands were red from lye water.

“Tunnel assignment.”

“Since when?”

“Since the governor got scared.”

Mira stared at the slate in his hand.

“Tavin.”

He hated that tone.

The same one she used before telling him they could not afford meat that week.

“I have to go.”

“You don’t.”

“They’ll dock my wages and beat me besides.”

“They already beat you.”

“That’s forge work.”

She looked down at the spear. “You don’t know how to use that.”

“Neither do half the wardens.”

Mira caught his wrist before he could move again.

“You remember father talking about the western roads?”

Tavin shrugged.

“The old stories.”

“He wasn’t telling stories.”

Rain slid off her hood onto his knuckles.

“He saw something down there during the flood years.”

“Our father saw ghosts in beer cups.”

“That’s easy for you to say now that he’s dead.”

Tavin pulled free harder than he meant to.

People turned to look.

A cart mule snorted steam beside them.

Mira lowered her voice.

“He came home with burns all over his chest. He woke up screaming for months. He said there were doors below the cistern road.”

“Everybody says that.”

“He said something was knocking.”

Tavin forced a grin he did not feel.

“Then I’ll tell it to stop.”

She did not smile back.

The militia barracks stood against the mountain wall beside the old chain lift. Rain hammered the slate roof. Tavin joined twenty other laborers under the entry arch.

Most were miners.

Two were dockhands.

One old woman carried a butcher’s cleaver through her belt and looked less frightened than the rest.

A captain came down the stairs fastening the straps on a scale coat.

Broad shoulders.

Grey in his beard.

Sword polished clean despite the weather.

Captain Varo.

Tavin had seen him before breaking strikes.

Varo looked over the crowd.

“You’re here because the city needs bodies.”

Nobody answered.

“You’ll obey tunnel commands. You’ll hold formation. You’ll carry lamps low. If we tell you to run, you run.”

The butcher woman raised a hand. “What are we hunting?”

Varo paused.

“Missing wardens.”

“Alive?”

“We’ll see.”

The answer settled badly across the group.

Varo pointed toward a weapons table.

“Take a blade or spear. No fighting over steel. Most of you will only slow us down anyway.”

A few nervous laughs escaped.

Tavin took a miner’s spike instead of the spear.

The weight felt better.

Something he understood.

An hour later they descended through the western maintenance tunnels.

The deeper roads were colder than the forge levels.

Water dripped constantly from the ceilings. White fungus clung to the mortar lines. Old warning sigils had been scratched into the walls with black pitch.

Some had been crossed out.

The air smelled wrong.

Not rot.

Not sewer gas.

Metal.

Like wet copper left too long in blood.

Tavin kept near the back while wardens carried shuttered lanterns ahead.

Their boots splashed through shallow runoff.

Nobody spoke much.

The silence made every noise stand out.

Breathing.

Buckles clicking.

Water hitting stone.

Somewhere deeper in the tunnel came a slow hollow knocking.

Three knocks.

Pause.

Three more.

Tavin glanced toward the others.

They heard it too.

Captain Varo raised a fist.

The column stopped.

Ahead stood a massive iron door half buried behind fallen masonry.

Green marks glowed faintly across its surface.

Old script.

The sort temple scholars painted onto funeral vaults.

One side of the door stood open wide enough for a cart.

The edges looked bent from inside.

Nobody moved.

Varo stepped closer.

One of the wardens gagged.

Tavin smelled it a moment later.

Blood.

Fresh.

Varo opened his lantern shutter.

The light spilled across the tunnel floor.

A body lay against the wall.

Road warden.

Helmet crushed inward.

The man’s chest had been opened from throat to stomach.

Not cut.

Peeled.

Something black glistened inside the ribs.

Tavin swallowed hard.

The knocking sounded again.

Closer now.

From beyond the door.

Varo pointed at four men.

“With me.”

Nobody volunteered.

He chose anyway.

Tavin ended up among them before he understood what was happening.

The captain noticed the panic in his face.

“You can hold a lamp?”

“Yes.”

“Then hold it steady.”

They stepped through the gate.

The chamber beyond looked older than the city above.

No mortar.

No support beams.

The walls had been carved straight from black stone veined with dull green metal.

The floor sloped downward into darkness.

Strange iron tracks ran through the center of the tunnel.

The knocking stopped.

Tavin raised the lantern higher.

There were handprints on the walls.

Bloody ones.

Human.

Dozens.

A warden farther ahead whispered, “Saints preserve us.”

Then something moved in the dark.

Fast.

One of the wardens screamed.

The sound cut off wet.

Lantern light swung wildly.

Tavin saw pale limbs.

Too many joints.

A face stretched tight over a skull with no eyes.

Teeth grinding together.

The creature slammed the warden against the wall hard enough to crack stone.

Varo drew his sword.

“Back!”

Nobody listened.

The laborers broke immediately.

Men crashed into each other trying to flee the tunnel.

The thing turned toward Tavin.

Its skin looked boiled.

Metal hooks pierced its shoulders.

Green light leaked from cracks in its chest.

It moved like a starving man lunging for bread.

Tavin swung the lantern.

Glass shattered across the creature’s face.

Flame burst over oil-soaked flesh.

The thing shrieked.

The sound scraped inside his skull.

Varo rammed his sword through its throat.

The blade snapped.

The creature grabbed the captain’s arm and twisted.

Bone punched through skin.

Varo dropped to one knee.

Tavin reacted before thinking.

He drove the miner’s spike into the green crack in the creature’s chest.

The metal sank deep.

Heat exploded up his arm.

The creature jerked.

Green light flashed bright enough to blind him.

Then the thing burst apart.

Not flesh.

Ash.

Black ash and sharp fragments of glowing stone.

The tunnel went silent except for Varo screaming through clenched teeth.

Tavin staggered backward.

His hands smoked.

The spike had fused to his palm.

Something burned beneath his skin.

He stared at the glowing green lines crawling across the back of his hand.

Captain Varo looked up at him with blood running down his sleeve.

Fear sat plain on the man’s face.

“What did you do?”

Tavin did not answer.

Because the knocking had started again.

Deeper under the mountain.

And this time it sounded like hundreds of fists.

## Chapter Two — Salt in the Blood

The fever hit before dawn.

Tavin woke strapped to a cot in the barracks infirmary with sweat soaking the blanket beneath him.

His right hand had been wrapped in stiff white bandages crusted yellow at the edges. The smell of boiled linen, vinegar, and old pus clogged the room.

Somebody nearby coughed thickly into a basin.

Rain hammered the shutters.

Tavin tried to sit up.

Pain shot from his palm to his shoulder.

“Lie down,” a woman said.

He turned his head.

An old surgeon stood beside the cot sharpening a bone saw with slow patient strokes. Her sleeves were rolled to the elbow. Burn scars climbed both forearms.

“What happened?” Tavin asked.

“You passed out.”

“My hand.”

“You touched deepstone.”

The surgeon dipped the saw into oil.

“You should be dead.”

That answer sat badly in his stomach.

He pulled against the straps.

“Take these off.”

“When the shaking stops.”

“I’m not shaking.”

She nodded toward his legs.

The blanket trembled visibly.

Tavin stared.

He had not noticed.

The surgeon set the saw aside.

“Name?”

“Tavin.”

“Family?”

“Sister.”

“No wife?”

“I’m sixteen.”

“That never stopped anyone in this city.”

She unwrapped his hand carefully.

Tavin clenched his jaw.

Green lines spread beneath the skin from the center of his palm.

Thin as roots.

The flesh around them looked bruised black.

The surgeon pressed one line with her thumb.

Pain flashed white through his arm.

He hissed.

“Can you feel that?”

“Yes.”

“Good.”

“How is that good?”

“Means the rot hasn’t reached the nerves.”

Tavin stared at her.

“Rot?”

She met his eyes plainly.

“You know those things under the mountain weren’t natural.”

“Yes.”

“The green stone inside them is older than the city. The temple calls it deepstone. During the flood wars they mined chunks from the lower chambers and used it to power lifts and pumps.”

She rewrapped his hand.

“Then people started changing.”

Tavin remembered the creature’s face.

The stretched skin.

The grinding teeth.

His stomach turned.

“What happens now?”

“That depends.”

“On what?”

“How much stone got into your blood.”

The infirmary door opened.

Captain Varo stepped inside wearing a sling over his ruined arm.

The room smelled colder when he entered. Wet leather. Rainwater. Steel oil.

He dismissed the surgeon with a nod.

She left without argument.

Varo dragged a chair beside the cot and sat carefully.

“You killed one.”

Tavin said nothing.

“Three wardens emptied crossbows into it before you struck.”

“I got lucky.”

Varo studied him.

“You know what the old kingdoms called those things?”

Tavin shook his head.

“Threshers.”

The captain leaned back slowly.

“They were made for tunnel war. Strong in tight spaces. Hard to kill. Easy to replace.”

“Made?”

“Not born.”

Tavin looked down at the wrapped hand.

“People?”

“Once.”

The rain outside thickened.

Water rattled against the shutters.

Varo lowered his voice.

“The governor wants the tunnels sealed and every witness silenced.”

That cut through the fever haze quickly.

“What?”

“You think the trade houses want panic? Half the city sits on tunnels built by dead kingdoms.”

Varo rubbed his beard with his good hand.

“They’ll call it a gas collapse. Missing laborers. End of story.”

“And us?”

“The surviving wardens stay quiet.”

Tavin understood then.

His mouth went dry.

“You came here to kill me.”

Varo did not answer for a moment.

Then he said, “I argued against it.”

“That’s comforting.”

The captain looked tired enough to sleep standing.

“Listen carefully, boy. The thing you touched reacted to you.”

“How?”

“You shattered it.”

“I stabbed it.”

“With iron.”

Varo leaned closer.

“Ordinary iron doesn’t break deepstone cores. We tried during the flood wars. Hammers cracked first.”

Tavin stared at him.

“So what are you saying?”

“I’m saying the stone inside you answered the stone inside it.”

The infirmary suddenly felt too small.

The smell of sickness thickened in his nose.

“I don’t want any of this.”

“Nobody asked.”

Varo stood.

“The governor’s men arrive by noon. If they find you here, you disappear.”

“Then why tell me?”

The captain flexed his ruined arm once and winced.

“Because I watched good men die in those tunnels twenty years ago while fat merchants argued over tariffs aboveground.”

He tossed a leather bundle onto the cot.

Inside sat a knife, a loaf of black bread, and a small purse of coin.

“There’s an old smuggler road beyond the tannery district. Follow the river south.”

Tavin stared.

“You’re helping me escape?”

“I’m helping myself sleep tonight.”

Varo paused at the door.

“One more thing. If the lines on your arm reach the shoulder, cut the limb off before the fever takes your mind.”

Then he left.

Tavin sat alone listening to rain and coughing.

He untied the straps himself.

His legs shook when they touched the floor.

The green lines had already crawled halfway to his wrist.

---

Mira slapped him hard enough to split his lip.

“You stupid bastard.”

They stood inside their apartment above the washhouse while steam drifted from the laundry vats downstairs. Wet cloth hung from ropes across the ceiling. The room smelled of soap, damp wood, and onion broth gone cold.

Tavin wiped blood from his mouth.

“I came to warn you.”

“You came here bleeding in broad daylight with city wardens searching half the district.”

“They haven’t found me.”

“Yet.”

Mira paced the narrow room.

She had changed from work clothes into an old brown dress patched at both elbows. Soap stains marked the fabric near the hem.

“You should’ve kept running.”

“I’m not leaving without you.”

She stopped.

“You think I can run south carrying what?”

She pointed around the apartment.

“One blanket? Half a sack of grain? We survive because I know people here.”

Tavin looked toward the window.

Grey rain covered the city beyond the glass.

“I can work.”

“You can barely stand.”

She noticed his hand then.

The bandage had soaked through.

“Tavin.”

He pulled the wrap loose.

Mira went pale.

The green lines now spread nearly to his elbow.

“What did they do to you?”

“I touched something underground.”

“That isn’t an answer.”

He told her.

Not everything.

Enough.

The gate.

The creature.

The deepstone.

Mira sat slowly at the table when he finished.

The apartment creaked around them.

Downstairs somebody laughed near the wash vats.

Normal sounds.

The sort that felt strange after seeing something impossible.

“Our father knew,” she said quietly.

“He knew enough to drink himself dead.”

“He tried to leave the tunnel crews after the flood wars.”

She rubbed her thumb over a crack in the tabletop.

“They wouldn’t let him.”

Tavin frowned.

“What do you mean?”

“He signed a guild oath.”

“Everybody signs guild papers.”

“Not like this.”

Mira stood and reached beneath the bed.

She dragged out a rusted lockbox.

The key hung around her neck.

Inside lay old papers wrapped in oilcloth.

Tavin recognized their father’s handwriting immediately.

Large ugly letters pressed hard enough to tear the paper.

Mira handed him one sheet.

The parchment smelled faintly of mildew and lamp oil.

At the bottom sat a dark red thumbprint beside the forge guild seal.

Tavin read aloud slowly.

“Workers assigned to lower excavation hold permanent silence regarding all discovered chambers, mechanisms, entities, substances, or rites under penalty of seizure and binding.”

He looked up.

“Binding?”

Mira handed him another paper.

A medical report.

Sketches of bodies.

Green crystal growths bursting through skin.

Tavin felt cold despite the steam in the room.

“Our father stole these,” she said.

“He wanted proof.”

“Proof of what?”

“That the governors kept mining the deep roads after the flood wars.”

Tavin looked back at the contract.

“Seizure and binding.”

“He said workers who talked disappeared.”

Outside, boots pounded through the street.

Voices shouted.

Mira crossed to the window carefully.

Two militia squads moved through the rain below.

Checking buildings.

Tavin grabbed the knife Varo had given him.

Mira shut the curtains.

“You need to leave now.”

“What about you?”

“I stay.”

“They’ll question everybody connected to me.”

“I know how to lie.”

He hated that she sounded calm.

“Tavin.”

She stepped closer and touched his cheek.

Her hands smelled of soap and wet linen.

“If father hid those papers, he thought they mattered.”

She pushed the documents into his hands.

“Find out why.”

He opened his mouth.

A fist slammed against the apartment door.

“Militia inspection!” somebody barked.

Mira shoved him toward the back window.

“Go.”

“I’m not leaving you.”

Another slam.

The doorframe cracked.

Mira grabbed his burned hand hard enough to hurt.

“You think dying here helps me?”

He froze.

The apartment suddenly felt very small.

The pounding downstairs.

The steam.

The rain.

The smell of soap and fear.

Mira kissed his forehead once.

Then she shoved him toward the window again.

“Move.”

Tavin climbed onto the back ledge just as the front door splintered open.

He heard men entering.

Mira shouting.

Boots.

Furniture overturning.

He dropped into the alley below and nearly collapsed when pain shot through his arm.

Green light flickered briefly beneath the skin.

He pulled his sleeve down and ran.

---

South of Kel Harrow the mountain roads narrowed into muddy trade paths hugging the cliffs.

Tavin walked until sunset before the fever forced him to stop.

Rain had faded into thin mist.

Pines crowded the roadside. Their wet needles smelled sharp and clean after the city.

He found shelter inside an abandoned charcoal burner’s hut.

The roof leaked.

The floor was dirt.

Better than the road.

He lit a small fire using resin chips and sat with his back against the wall shaking.

The green lines now reached his upper arm.

He could feel them moving sometimes.

Like worms beneath the skin.

He nearly cut the arm off twice that night.

Each time he lost nerve when the knife touched flesh.

Near midnight hoofbeats approached outside.

Tavin grabbed the knife and crouched beside the door.

The rider dismounted.

No militia challenge.

No shouted order.

Just boots crunching wet gravel.

Then a woman’s voice.

“You hold blades like a dock thief.”

Tavin froze.

The door opened.

A tall woman stepped inside carrying a hooded lantern.

Grey travel cloak.

Dark braided hair streaked with silver.

Scar across the bridge of her nose.

A curved sword hung at her hip.

She smelled of horse leather, cold rain, and medicinal herbs.

Her eyes settled immediately on his glowing arm.

“There you are,” she said.

Tavin tightened his grip on the knife.

“Who are you?”

“Sered.”

“That means nothing.”

“It means more than Tavin if we start counting names.”

She crouched near the fire without asking permission.

“You killed a thresher under Kel Harrow.”

“How do you know that?”

“Because every deep bell from here to the coast rang tonight.”

She pulled off her gloves.

Her left hand carried the same green lines beneath the skin.

Older.

Darker.

Tavin stared.

“You’re infected too.”

Sered snorted softly.

“That word annoys me.”

“What else should I call it?”

“A key.”

Rain tapped against the roof.

Tavin’s grip tightened again.

“If you came to drag me back, try it.”

“I didn’t ride two days through mountain rain to hand you to city clerks.”

She nodded toward his arm.

“You’re changing quickly.”

“So?”

“So if we leave it alone, the stone will cook your brain and wear your body like old boots.”

Tavin swallowed.

The fire popped between them.

“You know how to stop it?”

“Yes.”

“How?”

“By reaching the place where the bells began.”

He laughed once.

Short.

Tired.

“That sounds like another story drunk miners tell children.”

Sered leaned forward.

The firelight sharpened the scar on her face.

“Listen carefully, boy. There is a machine buried beneath these mountains older than every kingdom above them. Your governors woke pieces of it during the flood wars. They thought deepstone was fuel.”

She shook her head.

“It is memory.”

Tavin stared at her.

“You sound insane.”

“Maybe. Still alive though.”

She pulled a folded map from her cloak and spread it across the dirt floor.

Old roads.

Mountain marks.

Symbols Tavin did not recognize.

One location had been circled repeatedly in black ink.

“The Hollow Stair,” she said.

“What is it?”

“A path into the old engine chambers.”

“And why would I go there?”

“Because the bells are waking something below the mountains.”

Her eyes fixed on him.

“And because once the stone reaches your lungs, you won’t have enough mind left to choose anything.”

Tavin looked at his arm.

Green light pulsed faintly under the skin.

Outside, wind moved through the pines.

He thought of Mira.

Of the militia breaking the apartment door.

Of the creature in the tunnel.

“What happens if we do nothing?”

Sered answered immediately.

“The deep roads open.”

“And?”

“And every city built over them starts bleeding.”

The hut fell quiet.

Tavin stared at the map for a long time.

Then he said, “I don’t trust you.”

“Good.”

“You could be lying about all of this.”

“Yes.”

“You could kill me in my sleep.”

“That too.”

She sat back against the wall and closed her eyes.

“But if you stay here alone, you die before the week ends.”

The bluntness helped.

No speeches.

No promises.

Just weather and facts.

Tavin fed another resin chip into the fire.

“Suppose I come with you.”

Sered opened one eye.

“Then we head south at first light.”

“To where?”

“The coast.”

“Why the coast?”

“Because there’s a man there who once helped build the seals under Kel Harrow.”

She smiled without warmth.

“And he owes me a great deal of money.”

## Chapter Three — The Hollow Stair

Three weeks later Tavin killed a man for the first time.

The smuggler fell badly.

One moment he stood on the wagon roof with a hooked spear. The next he slipped in mule blood and cracked the back of his skull against the cliff road.

The sound stayed with Tavin.

A wet stone sound.

Short.

The body rolled twice before dropping into the ravine.

Nobody climbed down after it.

Sered wiped her sword clean on a dead mule’s harness.

“Move the carts,” she said.

Rain clouds hung low over the southern pass. Wind shoved cold mist through the cliffs. The ambush had lasted less than a minute.

Three smugglers dead.

One escaped.

Tavin’s hands still shook.

“You all right?” Sered asked.

“He’s dead.”

“Yes.”

“I didn’t mean to—”

“You hit him with a pry bar during a fight.”

She shoved a corpse off the road with her boot.

“He made the rest himself.”

The answer did not help much.

Tavin climbed onto the lead wagon and cut the surviving mule free.

His burned arm no longer hurt constantly.

That frightened him more.

The green lines had spread across his shoulder and upper chest. Sometimes they glowed through his shirt at night. Sometimes he heard distant bells while awake.

Sered claimed the stone sharpened certain senses before it hollowed the mind out.

So far she had been right.

He could hear wagon axles squeal half a mile away.

He could smell river mud before reaching water.

And twice now he had dreamed of tunnels he had never seen.

Black stairs.

Bronze doors.

A machine turning somewhere under the earth.

They reached the coast city of Vey two days later.

Tavin hated it immediately.

The sea smell hit first.

Rotting fish.

Salt.

Tar.

The harbor water looked thick enough to chew.

Ships crowded the docks beneath forests of rope and sailcloth. Workers hauled crates through muddy streets lined with gambling dens and warehouse shrines.

Vey sounded louder than Kel Harrow.

Everybody shouting over gulls and surf.

Sered led him uphill through the old quarter.

Stone houses leaned toward each other above alleys narrow enough to touch both walls with outstretched arms.

Laundry dripped overhead.

Children watched from windows.

A drunk sailor pissed into a gutter while singing off key.

They stopped outside a workshop marked with a faded brass sigil.

A circle crossed by three vertical lines.

Sered knocked twice.

Nothing.

She kicked the door.

“Open up, Bren.”

Bolts scraped inside.

The man who opened the door looked ninety years old until he moved.

Then he looked dangerous.

Thin.

Bald.

Left ear missing.

His right eye replaced with polished black glass.

Metal braces wrapped both knees beneath stained trousers.

The workshop behind him smelled of oil, seawater, and hot copper.

Bren stared at Sered.

“I heard you died.”

“You hear many stupid things.”

His glass eye shifted toward Tavin.

Then to the glowing veins visible above the boy’s collar.

Bren swore softly.

“You brought another one.”

“Can we come inside?”

“No.”

Sered shoved past him anyway.

The workshop overflowed with broken machinery.

Bronze cylinders.

Cracked lenses.

Half-disassembled pumps.

Metal fragments covered in old green stains.

Tavin noticed heavy locks on every shutter.

And salt circles painted around the floor drains.

Bren noticed him looking.

“They crawl through pipes if you give them room.”

Tavin hoped he meant rats.

Sered removed her cloak and sat at a cluttered table.

“We need access to the Hollow Stair.”

Bren barked a laugh.

“You need a grave plot.”

“The bells are active.”

“I know.”

He jabbed a finger toward the floor.

“The harbor chains rang three nights ago. Every compass in the district spun itself stupid.”

Tavin frowned.

“What are the bells?”

Bren stared at him.

“You haven’t told him?”

Sered shrugged.

“He’s still breathing. I considered that enough progress.”

Bren muttered something rude and limped toward a locked cabinet.

He returned with a bottle of dark liquor and three cups.

The drink smelled like paint thinner.

Tavin coughed after the first swallow.

Bren grinned.

“Good. Means your tongue still works.”

He sat opposite them.

“The bells are warning systems,” he said.

“Built by who?” Tavin asked.

“People who understood the deep roads better than we do.”

“That means nothing.”

“It means I don’t know.”

Bren rubbed at the scar under his missing ear.

“During the flood wars we broke into lower engine chambers under Kel Harrow. Governors wanted power for pumps and lifts. Deepstone carried force through metal better than coal steam.”

He looked toward Sered.

“We thought we found a machine.”

“What did you actually find?” Tavin asked.

Bren drank before answering.

“A prison.”

Silence settled.

Outside, harbor bells rang through the fog.

“The things in the tunnels,” Tavin said slowly. “Threshers.”

“Workers.”

Bren nodded.

“Servants bred for the roads. The old kingdoms fed prisoners and debtors into the deep engines. Deepstone changed them over time.”

He tapped the table near Tavin’s glowing arm.

“Like it’s changing you.”

Tavin looked away.

“There’s more,” Sered said.

Bren grimaced.

“There’s always more.”

She unfolded the stolen contract papers from Kel Harrow.

Bren read them.

His face tightened.

“Where did you get these?”

“My father stole them.”

“He was smart enough to be frightened then.”

Bren spread another diagram beside the contracts.

An enormous circular structure drawn in faded ink.

Layer upon layer of gears descending into darkness.

At the center sat a symbol shaped like an eye.

“The old engine beneath the mountains regulates the deepstone flow,” Bren said.

“When sealed properly, the roads sleep. When damaged, the stone leaks upward. Minds change. Bodies follow.”

Tavin pointed at the eye symbol.

“What’s that?”

Neither answered immediately.

Finally Sered said, “We don’t know.”

Bren snorted.

“She means she knows enough to avoid saying it aloud.”

The old man rose stiffly and crossed to the shutters.

He checked each lock before continuing.

“Something lives in the lower chamber. Maybe lived once. Maybe still does. The old records call it the Keeper.”

“Keeper of what?”

“The seals.”

Tavin felt tired clear through the bones.

Every answer uncovered two worse questions.

“What does any of this have to do with me?”

Sered leaned forward.

“Because you survived direct contact with raw deepstone.”

“So?”

“So people usually turn within hours.”

Bren nodded reluctantly.

“The stone bonded instead of consuming.”

Tavin laughed once.

“You both say terrible things very calmly.”

“Practice,” Bren said.

Sered slid a knife across the table toward Tavin.

“Cut your palm.”

He frowned.

“What?”

“Do it.”

Tavin sliced the edge of his thumb.

Blood welled dark red.

Then green light shimmered briefly beneath it.

The cut closed.

Fast.

Too fast.

Tavin stared.

Bren whispered a curse.

Sered looked grim instead of surprised.

“What does that mean?” Tavin asked.

“It means the stone has accepted you,” she said.

“That sounds bad.”

“It means the Hollow Stair may actually open.”

---

They left Vey before sunrise with supplies packed into two mules.

Bren insisted on joining them despite complaining the entire time.

“My knees hate mountain roads,” he muttered while tightening saddle straps.

“Stay home then,” Sered said.

“And let you idiots wake the lower chambers alone?”

He spat into the harbor.

“I prefer dying informed.”

The road inland crossed marsh country first.

Cold fog rolled over stagnant pools. Reed birds screamed at dusk. Mosquitoes whined around the mules.

Tavin slept badly.

The dreams grew sharper every night.

He saw giant gears turning through black water.

Stone pillars covered in green script.

A figure sitting motionless beside an enormous sealed gate.

Sometimes he heard a voice.

Not words.

More like pressure behind the eyes.

On the seventh night he woke standing outside camp barefoot in freezing mud.

The green lines across his skin glowed brightly enough to see.

Sered sat nearby watching him.

“You walked in your sleep,” she said.

Tavin looked toward the dark hills.

He knew without understanding how.

“The Stair is close.”

Sered’s expression tightened.

“Yes.”

Bren fed twigs into the fire.

“The boy hears the roads now.”

Tavin rubbed at his face.

His nose had bled during the night. Dried blood crusted beneath it.

“I keep seeing things.”

“That will worsen underground,” Bren said.

“Comforting again.”

The old man ignored him.

“Listen carefully, both of you. The Hollow Stair was built to restrict access to the lower engine chambers. The seals require three conditions.”

He held up crooked fingers one at a time.

“Blood carrying deepstone.

A living witness.

And a binding oath.”

Tavin frowned.

“What kind of oath?”

“The expensive kind.”

Bren reached into his coat and removed a small iron cylinder.

Inside sat rolled parchment sealed with black wax.

“Contract language,” he said.

“Old legal binding.”

Sered took the parchment carefully.

“The oath recognizes whoever speaks it as temporary keeper of the seal pathways.”

Tavin stared.

“You can make somebody guardian of an ancient underground prison with paperwork?”

Bren pointed at him.

“Civilization is mostly paperwork and murder.”

“Wonderful.”

The old man’s humor vanished.

“Listen. The oath matters because the engine responds to intent and authority. Loose language kills people.”

He unrolled the parchment.

The writing looked cramped and ugly.

“No breach allowed,” Bren continued.

“The speaker agrees to preserve the seal structure, prevent unauthorized passage, and contain hostile entities where possible.”

Tavin rubbed his eyes.

“What counts as breach?”

“Knowingly opening routes for personal gain. Damaging primary seals. Allowing uncontrolled spread of deepstone corruption.”

“And the penalty?”

Bren met his eyes.

“The engine takes the body.”

Tavin waited for a smile.

None came.

“The old keepers fused into the road systems after death,” Sered said quietly.

“Bits of them still maintain mechanisms below.”

He stared at her.

“You people say this like normal conversation.”

“It becomes normal after enough funerals.”

They reached the Hollow Stair the following evening.

The entrance sat hidden behind a waterfall spilling through a crack in the cliffs.

Cold spray soaked them immediately.

The stone door behind the water carried the same green markings Tavin had seen beneath Kel Harrow.

Only older.

Sharper.

The air smelled metallic.

Like blood on coins.

Tavin’s arm burned the closer he approached.

Green light pulsed beneath the skin.

Bren handed him the contract tube.

“You speak the oath. Nobody else here can.”

Tavin swallowed.

“I don’t even understand half of this.”

“You understand enough.”

“That seems irresponsible.”

Sered drew her sword.

“Something’s moving.”

Shapes shifted in the trees behind them.

Three.

Four.

More.

Threshers.

Their bodies looked less human than the one beneath Kel Harrow.

Longer limbs.

Bent spines.

Green light leaking through cracked skin.

One dragged a rusted chain behind it.

The sound scraped over stone.

Tavin’s pulse hammered.

“Why are they here?”

“Because they hear you,” Sered said.

The creatures began descending the slope.

Fast.

Bren shoved the parchment into Tavin’s hands.

“Read.”

“I can’t do this while those things—”

“Read!”

Sered met the first creature head on.

Her sword cut through its jaw.

Green sparks burst from the wound.

Another slammed into her sideways.

They crashed against the rocks.

Tavin unrolled the parchment with shaking hands.

The script crawled strangely before his eyes.

Then the letters settled.

As if something beneath the mountain wanted him to understand.

He began reading aloud.

The words tasted metallic.

Ancient.

Heavy.

The door vibrated.

Behind him gears groaned deep underground.

Threshers shrieked.

One lunged toward Bren.

The old man fired a compact pistol directly into its chest.

The shot blew green fire from its back.

Another creature grabbed Bren’s shoulder and bit through muscle.

He screamed.

Tavin kept reading.

The green lines across his body burned hot enough to make his vision blur.

The waterfall changed direction.

Water began flowing upward.

The stone door split down the center.

Darkness opened beneath the mountain.

A stairway descended farther than lantern light could reach.

Cold air rushed outward carrying smells of dust, machine oil, ancient graves, and something faintly sweet underneath.

Like flowers left too long beside a corpse.

The threshers froze.

Every creature turned toward the stair.

Then knelt.

Silence slammed across the cliffside.

Even the waterfall stopped roaring for one strange stretched moment.

Tavin’s final words echoed into the depths.

The contract parchment blackened in his hands.

Green marks spread across his palms.

Bren staggered against the rocks bleeding heavily from the shoulder.

Sered stared into the open stairway.

Fear sat plain on her face now.

“What did I just agree to?” Tavin asked.

Nobody answered.

Because something below had started climbing toward them.

Slow.

Measured.

One footstep at a time.

And with every step the bells beneath the mountains rang louder.

### Descent Into the First Vault

The stair went down farther than any mine shaft Tavin had ever seen.

The steps were too wide for ordinary feet.

Each tread carried grooves worn smooth by centuries of use. Water dripped steadily from somewhere deep below. The sound echoed upward through the shaft in slow hollow beats.

Bren wrapped his torn shoulder with strips cut from his coat.

Blood soaked through almost immediately.

“You still alive?” Sered asked.

“I object to the wording.”

He grimaced while tightening the knot with one hand.

The threshers remained kneeling near the entrance.

None crossed the doorway.

Tavin kept glancing back at them.

Their bodies twitched occasionally. Heads tilted in small jerking motions like hounds listening for whistles too distant for human ears.

“Why aren’t they attacking?” he asked.

Sered descended first with lantern raised.

“Because you spoke the keeper oath.”

“That doesn’t explain anything.”

“It explains enough.”

Tavin hated answers like that.

The deeper they traveled, the warmer the air became.

Not forge heat.

Different.

Wet.

Breathing.

The walls changed too.

Natural stone gave way to fitted black blocks joined without visible mortar. Thin green lines pulsed faintly between them. Tavin noticed symbols cut into the walls every fifty steps.

Hands.

Eyes.

Chains.

Spirals.

Some marks resembled legal stamps from guild warehouses aboveground.

That bothered him badly.

“How much of our laws came from this place?” he asked.

Bren barked a dry laugh.

“More than your governors would admit.”

The old engineer paused to catch breath.

“Ancient tunnel kingdoms ran on obligation. Every worker bound by debt. Every task recorded. The deepstone systems responded better to fixed agreements than loose commands.”

“That makes no sense.”

“Does to the machine.”

Tavin rubbed his glowing forearm.

“The machine thinks?”

“Nobody knows.”

“That answer keeps happening too.”

Sered smiled briefly.

“You’ll fit in underground.”

Three hours later they reached the first vault.

The chamber spread enormous beneath the mountain.

Tavin stopped walking without meaning to.

The place swallowed sound.

Bronze pillars thick as houses rose into darkness overhead. Massive chains disappeared upward beyond lantern range. Hundreds of iron cages hung suspended above a black pit in the center of the vault.

Most cages stood open.

A few still held bones.

The smell hit next.

Old metal.

Dust.

Oil.

And faintly beneath it, dried human waste trapped for centuries.

Bren lowered himself onto a broken stone block breathing hard.

“Transit prison,” he muttered.

Tavin stared at the cages.

“For who?”

“Workers.”

Sered approached the edge of the pit.

Green light drifted far below.

“The old kingdoms processed labor through these vaults before assigning tunnel sectors.”

Tavin looked around slowly.

“They built all this for prisoners?”

“For everybody.”

The answer landed heavier.

He moved toward one hanging cage.

Small scratches marked the bars.

Counting marks.

Hundreds.

Something inside the cage floor caught the lantern light.

A child’s shoe.

Leather hardened with age.

Tavin stepped back.

The deep roads felt less like forgotten magic now.

More like a factory left running after the owners died.

A low hum vibrated through the floor.

The chains above them shifted slightly.

Sered stiffened.

“Lanterns down.”

They shuttered the lights.

Darkness swallowed the chamber immediately.

Then Tavin heard movement.

Not threshers.

Human footsteps.

Several.

Approaching from the eastern archway.

Voices followed.

“Tracks came through here.”

“Check the lower rails.”

Militia.

Tavin recognized the accent.

Kel Harrow men.

Sered leaned close enough for him to smell cold sweat under the leather smell of her coat.

“Governor scouts.”

“How did they follow us?”

“Someone talked.”

Bren muttered a curse from the dark.

Lantern light bobbed through the eastern arch.

Five men entered the vault.

Tunnel gear.

Crossbows.

Scale armor coated in oilcloth against moisture.

Their leader carried a brass speaking horn marked with the governor’s seal.

“Search carefully,” he said.

“The infected boy is worth more alive.”

Tavin’s stomach tightened.

Sered touched the hilt of her sword.

“No fighting unless we must,” Bren whispered.

“Too loud down here.”

The scouts spread through the vault.

One climbed a rusted maintenance ladder toward the hanging cages.

Another moved toward the black pit.

The leader approached Tavin’s hiding place.

Close enough to hear armor straps creak.

The man smelled of damp wool and garlic.

Tavin held his breath.

The green lines beneath his skin pulsed suddenly.

The scout stopped.

He frowned into the dark.

“You hear that?”

Everybody froze.

A deep metallic sound rolled through the vault.

Not bells this time.

Grinding gears.

Huge ones.

The floor trembled.

One hanging cage began descending slowly toward the pit.

The scout near the ladder shouted.

“Captain?”

Something climbed out of the darkness below.

Hands first.

Human-looking hands covered in old scars and green mineral growth.

Then a face.

Blind white eyes.

Metal staples hammered through both cheeks.

The thing hauled itself onto the platform with painful jerking movements.

Another followed.

Then three more.

Not threshers.

These wore fragments of uniforms.

Ancient ones.

Bronze masks fused to rotten flesh.

The militia captain backed away.

“Formation!”

Too late.

One creature slammed into a scout hard enough to break his spine over a railing.

Another tore a crossbow away and beat its owner with the weapon until wood shattered.

The vault exploded into screaming.

Sered rose from hiding immediately.

“Move!”

They sprinted toward the western exit while the creatures tore through the scouts behind them.

Tavin risked one glance backward.

The militia captain tried blowing his speaking horn.

One of the ancient things bit his throat out before any sound emerged.

Blood sprayed across the bronze floor.

Tavin nearly slipped in it.

The western corridor narrowed sharply.

Pipes lined the ceiling.

Warm steam hissed through cracks.

Bren limped badly now.

His wounded shoulder left red streaks against the wall whenever he stumbled.

“You all right?” Tavin asked.

“No.”

Fair enough.

The sounds of killing faded behind them.

Then came another sound.

Voices.

Whispering.

Tavin slowed.

“You hear that?”

Sered did not answer immediately.

The whispers slid through the pipes around them.

Soft.

Overlapping.

Thousands of voices speaking too quietly to understand.

Bren crossed himself reflexively.

“Engine memory,” he said.

“That’s supposed to reassure me?”

“No.”

The corridor opened into a rail chamber.

Four ancient carts sat rusting beside twin metal tracks descending deeper underground.

Unlike everything else below, the rails looked maintained.

Recently.

Tavin crouched beside them.

Fresh scrape marks.

Oil residue.

“Something still uses these,” he said.

Sered nodded.

“Yes.”

Before he could ask more, the whispers stopped.

A voice replaced them.

Single.

Clear.

“Tavin.”

He spun.

Nobody stood there.

Bren and Sered watched him carefully.

“What?” Sered asked.

“Someone said my name.”

“I heard nothing,” Bren muttered.

The voice came again.

Closer now.

“Tavin Merrow.”

His father’s surname.

Nobody outside Kel Harrow used it.

Cold spread across his back.

“Who are you?” he whispered.

The rail cart nearest him lurched.

Ancient gears clicked alive beneath it.

Green light spread along the tracks.

Sered swore.

“The engine recognized him.”

“That seems bad,” Bren said.

“Everything here is bad.”

The cart door creaked open by itself.

Inside sat three metal seats covered in cracked leather.

The voice returned.

“Proceed to adjudication.”

Tavin stared.

“Adjudication?”

Bren looked genuinely frightened for the first time.

“We leave now.”

The floor shook again.

Heavy impacts echoed somewhere distant underground.

One.

Two.

Three.

Something huge moving.

Sered climbed into the cart.

“No better options.”

Bren hesitated.

Then another distant impact boomed through the chamber.

Dust rained from the ceiling.

The old man climbed aboard muttering curses.

Tavin entered last.

The door slammed shut behind him.

The cart launched forward immediately.

He nearly broke his teeth against the seat frame.

The rail chamber vanished behind them.

Green light streaked across tunnel walls while the cart screamed downhill.

Tavin gripped the seat arms hard.

The tunnel twisted through enormous underground spaces visible only in flashes.

Bridges.

Collapsed towers.

Pits full of machinery.

He saw lights moving far away in the dark.

Not lanterns.

Eyes.

The cart slowed after several minutes.

They emerged into another chamber.

This one looked cleaner than the others.

Less ruined.

White stone walls.

Bronze doors.

Long tables covered in dust.

Tavin smelled paper.

Dry old paper.

A hundred sealed cabinets lined the walls.

“What is this place?” he asked.

Bren stepped out slowly.

“Records office.”

“For a prison?”

“For an empire.”

The old engineer approached one cabinet reverently.

He wiped away dust.

Words emerged beneath.

CENSUS OF LABOR HOLDINGS.

Tavin stared.

“You’re telling me this entire underground kingdom kept lists of slaves.”

“Detailed lists.”

Bren opened a drawer.

Inside sat stacks of metal sheets etched with tiny writing.

Sered moved toward a large circular door at the far end of the chamber.

The door carried the eye symbol again.

Its surface pulsed faintly.

Tavin drifted toward another cabinet.

Something pulled at him.

Instinct.

He opened a drawer halfway down.

Inside sat a small bundle wrapped in cloth.

Not metal records.

Personal belongings.

A brass ring.

Two wooden dice.

And a folded note.

He opened it carefully.

The writing had faded badly.

Still readable.

If my sons live, tell them I did not volunteer.

Tavin swallowed.

That sentence hurt worse than the monsters.

Because it sounded familiar.

The voice returned again.

Closer than ever.

“Keeper candidate recognized.”

The circular door began unlocking.

Massive bolts withdrew one at a time.

Bren backed away.

“No.”

Sered drew her sword again.

“Tavin.”

“What?”

“When the door opens, do not agree to anything quickly.”

He stared at her.

“That advice feels late.”

The final bolt released.

The door opened inward.

Warm air flowed out carrying strange smells.

Machine oil.

Burned incense.

Human skin.

The chamber beyond glowed green.

At its center sat a throne connected to hundreds of cables and metal spines.

A figure occupied it.

Thin.

Wrapped in black cloth.

Its face hidden behind a bronze mask shaped like a calm human expression.

Wires disappeared directly into its spine.

The figure raised its head slowly.

Tavin realized with horror that the mask was breathing.

“Approach,” it said.

Its voice sounded layered.

Several voices speaking together.

Bren whispered, “Saints preserve us.”

Sered looked ready to run.

The figure focused entirely on Tavin.

“State your designation.”

He swallowed.

“Tavin Merrow.”

The mask tilted slightly.

“Lineage confirmed.”

Tavin frowned.

“What lineage?”

The figure ignored the question.

“Current surface authorities in breach of containment law. Seal integrity compromised across nine western sectors.”

Green light pulsed through the cables behind the throne.

“Keeper intervention required.”

Tavin glanced at Sered.

Her face had gone pale.

“Ask who it is,” she whispered.

Tavin looked back toward the throne.

“What are you?”

A long pause followed.

Then:

“Last active adjudicator.”

The answer explained nothing.

The figure rose from the throne with awful slowness.

Metal supports clicked beneath the robes.

Its body looked partly mechanical beneath the cloth.

Yet human hands emerged from the sleeves.

Old scarred hands.

The adjudicator stepped forward.

“Tavin Merrow. Descendant designation matches prior keeper auxiliary line.”

He blinked.

“My father worked mines.”

“Incorrect.”

The adjudicator stopped directly before him.

“Tavian Merrow Senior participated in seal maintenance under coercive contract authority during flood conflict period.”

Tavin felt cold.

“My father was here?”

“Yes.”

Bren closed his eyes briefly.

“Damn it.”

The adjudicator turned its masked face toward the old engineer.

“Engineer Bren Halvek. Recorded breach participant.”

Bren’s hand drifted slowly toward the pistol at his belt.

The adjudicator continued.

“Multiple unauthorized extractions. Seal degradation contributor.”

“Fair,” Bren muttered.

The adjudicator faced Tavin again.

“Containment failure probability exceeds acceptable threshold. Keeper authority transfer recommended.”

Tavin rubbed both hands over his face.

“No. Stop. Explain this like I’m a person instead of a machine part.”

Silence.

Then the adjudicator spoke differently.

Slower.

“As systems failed, auxiliary bloodlines were established among surface populations. Your father carried one.”

Tavin stared.

“Bloodlines for what?”

“For succession.”

The green lines beneath his skin burned.

“You want me to become whatever you are.”

“Partial correction.”

“That means yes.”

“Keeper role requires human discretion. Existing adjudicator functions deteriorating.”

The figure raised one trembling hand.

Beneath the sleeve the flesh looked cracked around embedded metal.

“Surface extraction activities damaged foundational seals. Hostile entities migrating upward.”

Images flashed suddenly across the chamber walls.

Cities.

Tunnel collapses.

Threshers climbing into streets.

Green crystal spreading through wells and reservoirs.

People tearing each other apart while glowing veins spread across their bodies.

Tavin staggered backward.

“What is this?”

“Projected outcomes.”

The images vanished.

Sered spoke quietly.

“Can the seals still hold?”

The adjudicator paused too long before answering.

“Unknown.”

Bren laughed bitterly.

“There’s the truth at last.”

The old engineer looked at Tavin.

“Now you understand why I drank.”

The chamber trembled violently.

Dust fell from above.

Alarm bells rang through the underground halls.

The adjudicator turned sharply toward the ceiling.

“Unauthorized breach detected.”

Sered swore.

“The governor’s people?”

“No,” the adjudicator said.

Its voice distorted.

“Deeper origin.”

A roar echoed through the distant tunnels.

Nothing human inside it.

The adjudicator looked back at Tavin.

“Decision window reduced.”

Tavin stared at the ancient figure connected to wires and engines.

At Sered.

At Bren clutching his wounded shoulder.

At the green light spreading through his own skin.

He thought of Mira alone in Kel Harrow.

Of the forge district.

Of cages hanging over black pits.

Of his father coming home burned and frightened.

“What happens if I refuse?” he asked.

The adjudicator answered immediately.

“Containment collapse likelihood increases beyond recovery thresholds.”

“And if I agree?”

A long pause.

Then:

“Unknown.”

# Glossary and World Lore

## Tavin

Sixteen-year-old forge laborer from Kel Harrow. Raised in the furnace district after his father died from injuries tied to the flood wars. Practical, stubborn, physically strong from industrial labor, and suspicious of authority. Exposure to deepstone altered his body instead of destroying it outright. The green lines spreading through him indicate partial synchronization with the old engine systems beneath the mountains.

Tavin’s worldview comes from labor districts and guild politics rather than scholarship. He notices hands, tools, hunger, wages, injuries, and power imbalances before philosophy. His instincts lean toward survival and protecting family, though the journey forces him into roles tied to ancient systems he barely understands.

## Mira

Tavin’s older sister. Laundry worker in Kel Harrow. Practical, sharp-tempered, financially careful, and more politically aware than Tavin. She preserved their father’s stolen documents after his death and understands how dangerous the governors truly are.

Mira represents the ordinary population trapped between trade guilds, industrial expansion, and hidden horrors buried beneath their cities.

## Captain Varo

Veteran militia officer from Kel Harrow. Participated in tunnel campaigns during the later flood wars. Lost men to deepstone exposure and secretly distrusts the city governors.

His broken arm came from direct combat with a thresher during the western tunnel expedition. Though loyal to the city in public, he quietly opposes attempts to suppress knowledge about the deep roads.

## Sered

Traveler, swordswoman, and former seal operative connected to the old deep-road containment systems. Partially altered by deepstone years earlier. Her body stabilized rather than collapsing, though the process left permanent changes.

Sered believes the old engine chambers must remain sealed at all costs. She uses blunt speech because she spent years dealing with governments, smugglers, miners, and desperate people in failing frontier settlements.

She understands more than she admits.

## Bren

Former engineer involved in deepstone extraction projects during the flood wars. Expert in seal mechanisms, tunnel systems, and ancient containment contracts. Cynical, deeply frightened of the old engine structures, and physically damaged from earlier exposure.

Bren helped seal sections of the deep roads decades earlier. He carries guilt over the deaths caused by industrial excavation beneath Kel Harrow.

## Deepstone

Green mineral substance found beneath mountain systems connected to the old engine chambers.

Deepstone conducts force, memory, and biological change. Exposure alters living tissue over time. Small controlled quantities can power machinery. Large-scale exposure reshapes bodies and minds.

Industrial governments exploited deepstone during the flood wars to power pumps, tunnel lifts, weapons, and subterranean infrastructure.

The substance appears semi-responsive to thought, intention, and ritual authority.

## Threshers

Tunnel-adapted servitor organisms created through deepstone alteration.

Originally human.

Threshers were used by ancient kingdoms for underground labor, warfare, and maintenance. Long exposure reshaped their bodies into durable forms suited for confined environments.

They recognize certain authority signals tied to the old seal systems and engine structures.

## Kel Harrow

Industrial mountain city built around mines, foundries, steam systems, and tunnel infrastructure.

Kel Harrow expanded rapidly during the flood wars when deepstone extraction allowed large-scale engineering projects beneath the mountains.

Publicly, the governors claim the western tunnels were abandoned because of structural instability and poison gas.

In truth, the lower roads connect to much older structures predating the city itself.

## The Flood Wars

Series of regional conflicts driven by collapsing river systems, dam failures, and industrial expansion.

Cities competed aggressively for underground resources and engineering advantages. Deepstone technology allowed governments to build massive pumping systems and subterranean transit networks.

Tunnel collapses, biological contamination, and unexplained disappearances became common near deep excavation sites.

Most official records were suppressed after the wars ended.

## The Bells

Ancient warning systems embedded throughout the deep-road networks.

The bells resonate through stone instead of air. Their ringing indicates seal disruption, engine activation, or movement within restricted chambers.

Different tones supposedly correspond to different levels of threat.

Most surface populations dismiss the bells as myth.

## The Hollow Stair

Sealed access route leading toward lower engine chambers beneath the mountain systems.

Protected by legal-magical oath mechanisms requiring:

- Deepstone-linked blood
- A living witness
- A binding contractual declaration

The Stair recognizes authority through both biological and ritual conditions.

Opening it risks awakening systems dormant for centuries.

## The Keeper

Entity or intelligence associated with the deepest seal systems.

Ancient records describe the Keeper inconsistently:

- Guardian
- Prisoner
- Operator
- Judge

Nobody in the current era fully understands whether the Keeper is alive, mechanical, collective, or something stranger.

Its existence is tied directly to the stability of the deep-road systems.

## Seal Contracts

Binding legal-ritual agreements tied to ancient engine governance.

The old kingdoms fused law, engineering, labor systems, and deepstone mechanics into unified enforcement structures. Contracts could trigger biological or mechanical consequences if broken.

Modern guilds copied fragments of these systems without fully understanding them.

Because of that, industrial governments still use diluted versions of seal contracts for labor secrecy and excavation control.

## Themes and Structural Notes

The story follows a hero’s journey framework shaped through industrial labor culture rather than noble fantasy.

Call to adventure:
The bell beneath Kel Harrow.

Crossing the threshold:
Entering the western gate tunnels.

Transformation:
Deepstone alteration.

Mentors:
Sered and Bren.

Revelation:
The deep roads are containment systems rather than forgotten ruins.

The larger journey remains unresolved at the end of Chapter Three. Tavin has accepted a role connected to the ancient seals without fully understanding the cost.

The open Hollow Stair marks the true beginning of his descent into the buried world beneath the mountains.
